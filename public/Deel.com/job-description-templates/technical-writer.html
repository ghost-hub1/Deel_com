How much does a Technical Writer make?

The exact salary of a Technical Writer can vary based on factors such as experience, location, industry, and the size of the organization. Below is a general overview of median salaries for Technical Writers in different regions.

  • North America: In North America, particularly the United States and Canada, Technical Writers typically earn a median annual salary ranging from $60,000 to $80,000.
  • Europe: In Europe, salaries for Technical Writers vary across countries. In Western European countries, median salaries can range from â‚¬40,000 to â‚¬60,000 or more annually.
  • Middle East and North Africa: In the MENA region, Technical Writer roles may have varying salary levels. Median salaries in countries like the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, or Saudi Arabia may range from $40,000 to $60,000.
  • Asia-Pacific: Technical Writer salaries in the Asia-Pacific region can vary widely. In countries like Australia, Singapore, or Japan, median wages range from $50,000 to $70,000.

Additional salary-related considerations 

While these figures provide a general indication, consider other factors when assessing Technical Writer salaries:

  • Certifications: Holding relevant certifications in technical writing, such as Certified Professional Technical Communicator (CPTC), may positively impact salary levels.
  • Experience level: Technical Writers with more experience and a diverse portfolio may command higher compensation.
  • Company size: Salaries may vary based on the size and industry of the organization. Larger enterprises or those in specialized fields may offer higher salaries.
  • Freelance opportunities: Some Technical Writers work as freelancers, and their income can vary based on the nature and volume of projects.

How to become a Technical Writer

While there isn't a standardized path to becoming a Technical Writer, several common steps can guide individuals aspiring to this role.

Common pathways to becoming a Technical Writer

Education 

Formal education in English, Communications, or a related field is a common starting point for becoming a Technical Writer. Many Technical Writers hold a bachelor's or master's degree. Coursework often includes technical writing principles, editing, and document design.

Portfolio development 

A portfolio showcasing your writing samples, including user manuals, guides, or online documentation, is essential for demonstrating your technical writing abilities to potential employers. A strong portfolio is often as important as formal education in the field.

Networking

Building a professional network in technical writing and related industries can provide insights, career advice, and potential job opportunities. Networking with other Technical Writers, attending industry conferences, and participating in online forums contribute to professional growth.

Internships and entry-level positions 

Gaining practical experience through internships, entry-level technical writing positions, or freelance opportunities is crucial for aspiring Technical Writers. These experiences provide opportunities to work on real-world projects, collaborate with subject matter experts, and build a professional reputation.

Technical Writer education

Education

A solid educational background in technical writing, English, communication, or a related field is typically beneficial for aspiring Technical Writers. Many Technical Writers hold a bachelor's or master's degree in areas such as:

  • Technical communication
  • English
  • Journalism
  • Communication studies
  • Information technology

Certifications

While not always mandatory, certifications can enhance a technical writer's credibility and demonstrate proficiency in the field. Relevant certifications include:

  • Certified Professional Technical Communicator (CPTC)
  • Technical Communication Fundamentals Certificate
  • Adobe Certified Professional â€“ Technical Communication Suite

Writing and editing skills

Technical writers must possess strong writing and editing skills. Consider taking courses or workshops focusing on technical writing principles, grammar, editing techniques, and style guides. Familiarity with tools like Adobe FrameMaker or MadCap Flare is also valuable.

Technical skills development 

Acquiring proficiency in using documentation tools, understanding various technologies, and learning to work with subject matter experts are crucial aspects of the role. Elements of the role that are worth developing further include:

  • Subject matter expertise
  • Documentation tools and software
  • Visual communication
  • User experience (UX) writing
  • Project management
  • Collaboration and communication skills

Alternative career paths

In some cases, individuals transition into the role of Technical Writer from different professional backgrounds. Understanding which roles align well with a transition to technical writing can help plan a successful career switch.

Roles that fit well with a transition to Technical Writer generally fall into these fields:

Content creation 

Professionals with experience in content creation, such as marketing or journalism, may transition into technical writing roles, leveraging their writing and communication skills.

Subject matter experts 

Individuals with expertise in a specific technical field, such as engineering or IT, may transition into technical writing to communicate complex information to a broader audience.

Copywriting

Professionals with a background in copywriting can seamlessly transition into the role of a Technical Writer. Their skills in crafting clear, concise, and engaging content can be applied to creating user manuals, technical documentation, and instructional guides. 

Journalism

Individuals with a journalism background may excel in conducting interviews with subject matter experts, gathering information, and presenting it in an informative and engaging format.

Engineering/technical background

Professionals from an engineering or technical background may excel in translating technical jargon into accessible language for a non-technical audience, ensuring that documentation is both accurate and understandable.

What is a technical writer? 

A Technical Writer is a professional figure who specializes in creating clear and concise documentation that makes complex technical information accessible to a non-technical audience. They produce various types of content, such as user manuals, instruction guides, FAQs, online help systems, and software documentation.

Is technical writing an IT job? 

No, technical writing is not strictly an IT job, although it often intersects with the IT field. Technical writers work in various industries, including technology, engineering, healthcare, finance, and more. They create documentation for products, services, and processes, regardless of the specific industry or field.

What are the basics of technical writing? 

The basics of technical writing involve:

    • Simplifying complex concepts for a target audience
    • Creating structured and organized documents
    • Using clear and concise language
    • Incorporating visuals like diagrams or screenshots
    • Adhering to style guides and formatting standards
    • Collaborating with subject matter experts
    • Ensuring accuracy and consistency in information
    • Considering user needs and usability

Does a technical writer need a degree? 

While a formal degree can be beneficial, it is not always a strict requirement to become a technical writer. Relevant degrees in fields like English, Communications, Journalism, or any related discipline can provide a solid foundation in communication and writing. However, practical experience, a strong portfolio showcasing writing samples, and expertise in the subject matter are also essential.
